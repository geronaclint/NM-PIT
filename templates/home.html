Numerical Differentiation

In many engineering and scientific applications, we need to compute derivatives of functions — but the function may only be known at discrete points, or the analytical derivative may be too complex to obtain.

Numerical differentiation approximates derivatives using finite differences: small perturbations in the function's input. While straightforward, these approximations carry truncation error that depends on the step size \(h\).

Richardson Extrapolation is a powerful technique that combines multiple finite-difference estimates at different step sizes to systematically cancel error terms. The result is a dramatically more accurate derivative approximation — often achieving near-machine-precision accuracy.

Central Difference
\[f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x-h)}{2h}\]
Richardson Combination
\[D_j = \frac{4^j \, D_{j-1}(h/2) - D_{j-1}(h)}{4^j - 1}\]
